<big><u>Definition</u></big>
A **basis** is defined as a <u>linearly independent</u> set of vectors that <u>spans</u> an entire space.
Another way of thinking of the span part is that $Span(\vec{v_{1}}, \dots, \vec{v_{k}}) = R^n$
See [[Linear Independence]] and [[Span]].

The **Standard basis vectors** (e.g., {$\vec{e_{1}},\ \vec{e_{2}},\ \vec{e_{3}}$} for $R^3$) are the most straightforward example of a basis.

Example 1: The following vectors are the standard basis vectors for $R^3$
$$
\begin{align}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
0 \\
0
\end{bmatrix},
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
1 \\
0
\end{bmatrix},
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
0 \\
1
\end{bmatrix}
\end{align}
$$

<big><u>Example problem</u></big>
Prove that the set $B=\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}-1\\2\end{bmatrix},\begin{bmatrix}1\\5\end{bmatrix} \right\}$ is a basis for $R^2$.
